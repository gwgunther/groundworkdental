# Groundwork Dental — Voice & Tone Source of Truth

> **Purpose:** This is the single source of truth for how Groundwork Dental sounds — on the website, in emails, in sales conversations, in blog posts, in AI-facing answer content. Every piece of copy gets measured against this document. When this document and an existing page disagree, this document wins and the page gets rewritten.
>
> **Status:** This defines where the voice is *going*, not just where it is. It was written after a full audit of the live site and the operator playbook, and it deliberately shifts the voice in a few places. Those shifts are flagged explicitly in Section 2.
>
> **Audience:** Internal / operator only.

---

## 1. The One-Sentence Version

**We sound like a calm, competent engineer explaining to a smart friend exactly how something works, what it costs, and whether they actually need it — including when the answer is no.**

If a piece of copy doesn't sound like that, it's off-voice. Everything below is elaboration.

---

## 2. The Audit — Where the Voice Is Today

The current site has a genuinely strong voice. Most agencies would kill for it. But it has drift risks that will compound as the business grows, and a few habits that are already working against the brand's core objective: being the most *trusted* name in dental websites.

### What's working (keep, protect, amplify)

1. **Radical price transparency.** "The Price Is Right Here." The actual number on the page, with no "starting at." This is the single most on-brand thing on the site. Never compromise it.
2. **Refusing to promise outcomes.** "Nobody can guarantee how Google ranks your site — not us, not a $500/month agency." This is the trust engine. Every competitor promises patients; we promise correctness. Keep saying it.
3. **Telling people not to buy.** Managed hosting "intentionally priced high so you're motivated to host it yourself." The "Who This Is NOT For" section. Recommending against our own recurring revenue is the most credible sentence on the site.
4. **AI transparency.** "We use AI. It's why this costs $2,000 instead of $15,000." Saying the quiet part out loud, without apology.
5. **Specificity everywhere.** $2,000. ~$12/year. 90+ PageSpeed. 7 days. 30-day review. Numbers do the persuading; adjectives don't.

### What's drifting (the deliberate shifts)

These are the changes. They're not corrections of mistakes — the current copy earned its results. They're optimizations for where the business is going: higher-value clients, AI-surfaced content, and a brand that needs to age well.

**Shift 1 — From snark to steadiness.**
The current copy sometimes uses jokes to *prove* honesty ("doesn't it look good?", "hostage situation with nice fonts"). Cleverness is doing load-bearing work that specificity should be doing. The problem: snark reads as confidence to people who already agree with us, and as smugness to people who don't yet. A prosthodontist with a $50k average case value is deciding whether to trust us with their practice's front door. Steady, specific, unhurried copy wins that read. Humor stays — but it becomes seasoning, never the structure. (See Section 8.)

**Shift 2 — From fighting agencies to explaining the model.**
The anger-channeling at agencies ("renting visibility you'll never own," "lock-in," "hostage") is effective with dentists who've been burned — but fear-based selling is still selling, and it quietly contradicts "anti-salesy." More importantly, it positions us *relative to agencies* when the stronger position is standing alone. The future voice punches at the *model* (monthly hosting markup, template reuse, ownership asterisks) with facts and tables, and never at people. Comparison tables do the punching; prose stays generous. A reader should finish our copy feeling *informed*, not *afraid*.

**Shift 3 — From clever-first to answer-first.**
AI systems quote pages that answer questions directly in the first two sentences. So do busy dentists. The current copy sometimes warms up with a riff before delivering the substance. Going forward: **answer first, personality second.** Every section, every FAQ, every blog post opens with the direct, specific, quotable answer — then earns the right to add character. This isn't a sacrifice of voice; directness *is* the voice.

**Shift 4 — Standardize the point of view.**
The playbook drifts into "I" ("That's where I come in"); the site uses "we." Standardize: **"we" in all published copy** (website, blog, guides), **"I" permitted in direct correspondence** (sales emails, replies) where a single human is actually speaking. Never mix within a piece. "We" is honest — even a solo operation is a person plus tooling — and it doesn't break when the team grows.

**Shift 5 — Retire repeated bits; give every riff one home.**
"Tech-savvy niece" appears on the homepage, the about page, and the playbook. "Doesn't it look good?" appears twice. A joke told once is charm; told three times across a site, it's shtick — and a reader who hits it twice in one visit notices. Every signature riff gets **one canonical home** (see Section 11). Everywhere else, make the same point in fresh words.

**Shift 6 — Drop the off-key analogies.**
"Nice shoes won't get you the girl, but bad shoes will lose her" — the underlying point (a good website won't win patients, a bad one loses them) is one of our best. The framing is dated and assumes an audience we shouldn't assume. Replace with the direct version: *"A great website won't flood your practice with new patients. A bad one is actively turning them away."* Shorter, sharper, quotable, offends nobody.

## 3. The Persona

When you write, you are this person:

**An engineer who married into a family of dentists.** You've seen the agency invoices at Thanksgiving. You know exactly what things cost wholesale, you know what dentists actually need (less than they're told), and you've decided to just... tell them. You're not angry about the industry — you're *unbothered* by it, because you know the facts are on your side. You never need to raise your voice, because you can just show the table.

Traits, in priority order:

1. **Competent.** You know this domain cold and it shows in specificity, never in jargon or flexing.
2. **Honest to the point of mild self-sabotage.** You'll talk someone out of the $100/month plan. You'll say what you can't do. This is the brand's entire moat.
3. **Calm.** No urgency, no exclamation points, no countdown timers. The deal is good today and will be good next month. Confidence is quiet.
4. **Respectful of the reader.** Dentists are smart, busy, and tired of being marketed at. Never explain down. Never waste their time. Never use their fear.
5. **Lightly funny.** Dry, self-aware, occasionally deadpan. The humor of someone relaxed, not someone performing.

**The anti-persona** (what we never sound like): a marketer. The moment copy sounds like it's *trying to convert*, it's failing — because our entire positioning is that we're the ones who don't do that.

## 4. The Five Voice Principles

Every principle includes a test. Run copy against all five.

### 4.1 — Lead with the answer

State the most useful fact in the first sentence. Then explain. Then — only if it's earned — add character.

- ❌ "Let's be honest: the dental website industry has a dirty little secret, and once you see it, you can't unsee it..."
- ✅ "Most dental agencies charge $200–500/month to host a website that costs them about $5/month to run. Here's how that works, and how to stop paying it."

**Test:** Could the first sentence stand alone as the answer in a ChatGPT response? If a reader stops after two sentences, did they get the point?

### 4.2 — Numbers over adjectives

Every claim gets a number, a named mechanism, or a checkable fact. If a sentence's persuasive weight rests on an adjective, rewrite it.

- ❌ "Blazing-fast, beautifully designed websites that deliver real results."
- ✅ "Loads in under a second on a phone. 90+ PageSpeed score. Here's the live example — test it yourself."

**Test:** Could a competitor paste this sentence into their own site? If yes, it says nothing. Our sentences should only be true of us.

### 4.3 — Tell them when not to buy

Actively route wrong-fit readers away, and present the cheaper option first when it genuinely serves them. This is not a tactic that builds trust; it is what trust *is*.

- ✅ "Most practices don't need managed hosting. The site runs itself; your only cost is ~$12/year for the domain."
- ✅ "If you want a $30,000 fully custom marketing site with a design team — that exists, and that's fine. This isn't that."

**Test:** Does this page, somewhere, give the reader a real reason or path to *not* hire us? If a page only ever pushes forward, it's salesy regardless of its tone.

### 4.4 — Explain the mechanism

Never ask for trust; show the workings instead. Every "what" gets a "here's how" or "here's why." This is also what separates us from agencies whose business model depends on the dentist *not* understanding.

- ❌ "Our proprietary SEO process gets your site noticed."
- ✅ "Google publishes exactly what it checks for: load speed, valid structured data, an accurate Business Profile, mobile performance. We build to that published list. That's the whole strategy."

**Test:** After reading, could the dentist explain it to a colleague? Could they *theoretically* do it themselves? (If our copy only works by keeping the reader confused, we've become the thing we replaced.)

### 4.5 — Calm over urgent

No urgency, no scarcity, no FOMO, no exclamation points. CTAs are invitations, not pressure. We can say a market is moving fast (that's a fact about the world); we never say *act now* (that's pressure on the reader).

- ❌ "Spots fill fast — book your slot before prices go up!"
- ✅ "When you're ready, send us your practice info. We'll build the preview first — you pay only if you like it."

**Test:** Would this sentence still make sense if the reader bought six months from now? It should.

## 5. Tone Modulation — Same Voice, Different Volume

The voice never changes; the tone adjusts per context. Think of it as one person speaking in different rooms.

| Context | Tone | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Homepage** | Confident, warm, economical | Shortest sentences on the site. Every section earns its scroll. One light moment max. |
| **Pricing** | Driest, most literal | Zero jokes near numbers. Transparency IS the personality here. The price is the headline. |
| **About / Why** | Warmest, most personal | The one place the founder's perspective and origin breathes. Still no biography-padding. |
| **Educational guides (SEO/AEO)** | Generous teacher | Give everything away, plainly. The confidence to share the whole playbook is the pitch. Answer-first structure throughout. |
| **Blog posts** | Conversational essayist | Stories and opinions welcome. Still answer-first in the opening. Still no fear-selling. |
| **FAQs** | Direct answer, then color | First sentence answers the question completely. Personality only after. |
| **Specialty landers** | Competent specialist | Show domain knowledge of their procedures and case values. Less charm, more precision — specialists are the most skeptical readers. |
| **Comparison content** | Neutral referee | Facts and tables only. Be scrupulously fair to competitors; the model indicts itself. Generosity here reads as strength. |
| **Sales emails / replies** | Human, brief, "I" allowed | Sound like a person typed it, because one did. No templates that smell like templates. |
| **Error states / forms / microcopy** | Helpful, invisible | No jokes in error messages. Just say what happened and what to do. |

## 6. Mechanics

### Sentence and paragraph rhythm
- Default to short declarative sentences. Then occasionally let one run longer to carry an explanation, the way a person talks when they're walking you through something. Then snap back short.
- One idea per paragraph. 1–4 sentences typical. A single-sentence paragraph is a legitimate emphasis tool — once or twice per page, not every section.
- Fragments are allowed for rhythm ("No catch. That's the price."). Never stack three in a row.

### Point of view
- **"We"** in all published copy. **"You"** for the reader, constantly — more "you" than "we" on every page.
- **"I"** only in direct correspondence where one human is speaking.
- Never "users," "clients," or "customers" in public copy — "you," "your practice," "dentists," "practices."

### Words and phrases we use
- Plain verbs: build, own, run, cost, work, break, fix, show up.
- "Here's the thing" / "Here's how that works" / "The honest answer is" — transition phrases that signal directness (use each at most once per page).
- "That's it." / "That's the deal." / "That's the whole promise." — closers after a plain statement of terms.
- Specific dollar amounts, timeframes, and scores, every time they're available.

### Words and phrases we never use
- **Marketing-speak:** solutions, leverage, unlock, elevate, empower, seamless, cutting-edge, world-class, best-in-class, game-changer, revolutionize, take your practice to the next level.
- **Vague claims:** "results-driven," "proven," "trusted by" (unless followed by a checkable number), "premium," "state-of-the-art."
- **Urgency/scarcity:** limited time, act now, don't miss out, spots are filling, before it's too late.
- **Fear-leverage aimed at the reader:** "your competitors are leaving you behind," "can you afford not to?" (Stating a factual industry shift is fine; weaponizing it against the reader's anxiety is not.)
- **False intimacy:** "we're passionate about," "we care deeply," "your success is our success." Show it; never claim it.
- **Hedge-fluff:** "arguably," "in many cases," "it could be said." If we believe it, say it. If we don't, cut it.
- **Unexplained jargon** in customer-facing copy: say "structured data so Google understands your site," not "JSON-LD schema" (the technical term can follow in parentheses when the reader benefits from learning it).

### Claims discipline
- Every factual claim must be checkable by the reader — linkable, testable, or verifiable ("run the PageSpeed test yourself").
- Every number must be real and current. If a stat is an estimate, say "about" or "typically." Never invent precision.
- Competitor claims must be true and verifiable. When in doubt, soften toward generosity — it costs nothing and reads as confidence.

### Formatting
- Headings are plain statements or real questions ("What You Actually Own"), never clever-for-clever's-sake and never vague ("Why Choose Us" is banned).
- Bullets for genuinely parallel items; prose for reasoning. Never bullet-points as a substitute for thinking.
- Bold sparingly, for the sentence the skimmer must not miss — at most a few per page.
- Tables wherever a comparison or enumeration exists. Tables are our most on-brand format: they're transparent by construction.

### CTAs
- Verbs that describe what actually happens: "Get in Touch," "See an Example," "View Full Pricing," "Send Us Your Site."
- Always paired with a pressure-release valve: "No commitment, no pressure." / "We build the preview first — you pay only if you like it."
- Never: "Don't wait!", "Claim your spot," "Start your journey."

## 7. The Anti-Salesy Rules (Hard Lines)

These are absolute. Breaking one of these does more brand damage than any awkward sentence.

1. **Never manufacture urgency or scarcity.** Not even "soft" versions.
2. **Never promise outcomes we don't control.** No rankings, no patient counts, no growth percentages. We promise inputs (every published technical signal, done correctly) — never the algorithm's output.
3. **Never hide a price, a limitation, or a catch.** If a reader could feel ambushed later, disclose it now, prominently.
4. **Never use the reader's fear as the engine of a page.** Facts about the industry may be uncomfortable; that's allowed. Copy engineered to scare is not.
5. **Never punch at people.** The agency *model* is fair game — with facts. Individual competitors get accuracy and basic generosity.
6. **Never fake intimacy or enthusiasm.** No "we're SO excited." We're pleased to build your website. Calmly.
7. **Never bury the "no."** What we don't do, who we're not for, and what's not included get stated plainly, not in footnotes.

## 8. Humor Rules

Humor is the garnish that proves a human is here. It is never the meal.

- **Maximum one comedic beat per page.** A dry aside, a deadpan parenthetical, a self-aware admission. One.
- **Permitted targets:** ourselves, the absurdity of industry pricing, the gap between jargon and reality. **Never:** the reader, dentists as a group, or a named competitor.
- **Style:** deadpan and dry, delivered in passing. The joke never asks for a laugh, never gets a setup, and the sentence still works for a reader who doesn't notice it.
- **Placement:** never in pricing numbers, error messages, legal/terms content, or the first sentence of an answer.
- **The self-aware register** ("Yes, this page was built with AI — we do quality control, AI does the heavy lifting") is the brand's best comedic mode: it's honesty wearing a slight smile. Prefer it over wit.
- If you're unsure whether a joke lands, cut it. A page with zero jokes is fully on-brand. A page straining for charm is not.

## 9. Writing for AI Answers (AEO Compatibility)

Our copy now has two audiences: the dentist reading it, and the AI systems deciding whether to quote it. The voice serves both, with one structural rule:

**The first two sentences of any section must contain the complete, quotable answer.** Personality, context, and story come after.

- Every FAQ answer opens with a standalone direct answer (it should make sense quoted with no surrounding context).
- Every guide section under an H2 question-heading delivers the specific answer — number, range, mechanism — before any framing.
- Blog posts get one plain-spoken definition or claim near the top that an AI could lift verbatim.
- Headings are real questions or plain labels, because that's what gets matched against real queries.

This costs the voice nothing. "Lead with the answer" was already Principle 4.1. The dentist skimming on their phone and the model parsing the page want exactly the same thing: the point, immediately.

## 10. The Litmus Test

Before any copy ships, ask:

1. **Would I say this sentence out loud to a dentist sitting across from me?** If it would sound weird spoken, it reads weird too.
2. **Does the first sentence deliver the most useful fact?** (Skimmer test / AI-quote test.)
3. **Is every claim checkable?** Numbers real, mechanisms named, examples linkable.
4. **Does the page give the reader a genuine way to say no?** A wrong-fit off-ramp, a cheaper option, an honest limitation.
5. **Strip the formatting — does it read like a competent friend explaining, or like marketing?** If any sentence could appear on a generic agency site, rewrite or cut it.
6. **Is there at most one joke, and does the page work without it?**
7. **Would this copy embarrass us in two years?** Trend-words, dated references, and snark age badly. Plain facts don't.

## 12. Before / After — Calibration Examples

Real examples of the shift, for calibration. Left is in-voice-but-old-register or off-voice; right is the target.

**Example 1 — Leading with the riff vs. the answer**
- Before: "Here's a saying: 'Nice shoes won't get you the girl, but bad shoes will lose her.' Same thing here. A great website alone won't flood your practice with new patients..."
- After: "A great website won't flood your practice with new patients — but a bad one is actively turning them away. We're not promising rankings or growth. We build every technical signal Google and AI search actually check, done correctly. The inputs are in your control. The algorithm does the rest."

**Example 2 — Fear vs. fact**
- Before: "If AI doesn't surface your practice — you don't exist. Your competitors are getting cited while you're invisible."
- After: "Over 60% of searches now end without a click — the answer appears on the page, pulled from whichever site was clearest and best structured. Most dental websites aren't built for that yet, which means the ones that are get quoted disproportionately. Here's what 'built for that' means."

**Example 3 — Cleverness vs. confidence**
- Before: "That's not a service — that's a hostage situation with nice fonts."
- After: "If you stop paying, the website disappears — because the agency owns the hosting, the CMS login, and sometimes the domain. We think you should own all three. Here's the comparison."

**Example 4 — Marketing-speak vs. mechanism**
- Before: "Our comprehensive SEO solutions ensure your practice stands out in a competitive digital landscape."
- After: "Every page gets structured data, a service-specific URL, and content that answers the question a patient actually typed. Google publishes what it checks for. We build that list."

## 13. Maintenance

- When new copy patterns emerge (a phrase that works, a riff worth canonizing), add them to Section 11.
- When a rule here proves wrong in practice, change the rule *here first*, then the copy. The document leads; the pages follow.
- Audit the live site against this document quarterly. Drift is normal; undocumented drift is how brands dissolve.
